Barely the size of your thumb and twice as mean. They ride dragonflies and moths into battle, wage tiny wars over dewdrops and interesting pebbles, and hold grudges for generations over insults that no one else remembers. Individually harmless. In swarms, they've been known to blind a horse, steal every button off a man's coat while he wears it, and lead entire caravans into bogs.
Special: Wings, Invisibility in moonlight
Close, Reach, Messy
Armor 1
Squat and leathery, with a permanent scowl and a cap dyed the color of old blood. They dip their hats in the gore of their kills to keep the color fresh — if it ever dries out, they die. This makes them frantic, eager murderers. They stomp about in heavy iron boots and swing their pikes with a ferocity that belies their size. Don't let the stature fool you. A redcap will gut a knight as happily as a rabbit.
Special: Iron boots

Armor 0
It looks like a lost child. It cries like one, too — big wet eyes and a trembling lip. Every protective instinct you have says to help. But the milk curdles when it's near. The fire gutters. The dog growls at nothing and then whimpers and hides. The changeling was left in place of something stolen, and it will hollow out a household from the inside — souring love, spoiling food, breaking bonds — until there's nothing left worth taking.
Special: Illusion of innocence
Close, Reach
Armor 2
She tends a garden of things that should not grow — teeth-flowers, memory-vines, roses that bloom only when someone nearby is lying. The bramble hag was a dryad once, or so the story goes, but she loved her oak so fiercely that when it was felled, the grief twisted her into something new. Now she cultivates spite and trades in secrets, and the forest bends itself into a maze around her home.
Special: Barkskin, Fey Sight
Near, ignores armor
Armor 1
In the deepest hollows of the feywood, where the canopy is so thick that noon looks like midnight, a vast fungal network pulses beneath the leaf litter. At its center sits the Sovereign — a towering mass of luminous caps and dripping mycelium that thinks in seasons and speaks through the fruiting bodies scattered across miles of forest floor. It is not hostile, exactly. It simply considers all flesh to be excellent fertilizer.
Special: Fungal hivemind, Regeneration in darkness
Close, Messy
Armor 2
Wolves made of twisted wood and living thorn, grown rather than born in the briarpatch warrens of the deep fey. They don't eat — they plant. A kill is dragged into the thicket and left to feed the roots. Where they hunt, the forest follows. Whole roads have been swallowed by briarwolf territory in a single season. They run in perfect silence except for the soft clicking of wooden joints.
Special: Bark and thorn hide
Close, Reach, Forceful
Armor 3
It walks between the trees without a sound — hooves on moss, antlers parting branches that seem to bow aside. The Erlking Stag is not a beast to be hunted. It is a judge. Those who enter the deep woods with ill intent find it watching from the treeline, and those who do not turn back find themselves tried by laws older than language. Its eyes hold the light of a court that sits in no castle.
Special: Fey nobility, Aura of silence

"Life is not a finite list of things that you check off before you're allowed to die," Harry said firmly. "It's life, you just go on living it. If I'm not doing those things it'll be because I've found something better." Dumbledore sighed. His fingers drummed on a clock; as they touched it, the numerals changed to an indecipherable script, and the hands briefly appeared in different positions. "In the unlikely event that I am permitted to tarry until a hundred and fifty," said the old wizard, "I do not think I would mind. But two hundred years would be entirely too much of a good thing." "Yes, well," Harry said, his voice a little dry as he thought of his Mum and Dad and their allotted span if Harry didn't do something about it, "I suspect, Headmaster, that if you came from a culture where people were accustomed to living four hundred years, that dying at two hundred would seem just as tragically premature as dying at, say, eighty."
